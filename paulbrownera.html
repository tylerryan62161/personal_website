Paul Brown

BIO

Paul Brown, founder and initial head coach of the Bengals, is one of the greatest coaches and one of the most influential figures in the history of the NFL. And yet, even among Bengals fans, this is a well forgotten fact. Most modern fans would say Bill Belichick, who authored the greatest dynasty the modern NFL has ever seen, is the greatest coach in the league's history. What does Bill think?

“He’s the greatest coach in the history of professional football, clear and simple. Everything I do today, Paul Brown did. It all started with Paul Brown. He took football from being a sport to a profession.” - Bill Belichick

Innovations:

  • Created the playbook
  • First to use 40 yard dash time to evaluate functional speed
  • First to value speed over strength
  • Invented the draw play - a delayed handoff to the running back
  • Created the pocket - area behind the offensive line for QBs to throw
  • Helped create the West Coast offense - most common offense used today
  • First to hire assistant coaches full time
  • First to designate assistant coaches to each position
  • First to teach schemes in classroom setting and test for retension
  • First to have strict organization of practices
  • First to analyze game film to make improvements or identify opponents' weaknesses
  • First to create detailed system to scout players and improve team drafts
  • First to utilize a practice squad - fringe players to develop
  • First to call plays to the QB using hand signals
  • Integral in developing the face mask
  • First to test and value intelligence in his players

Accomplishments:

  • Found success as a player while being outclassed athletically
  • Revived and made dominant his high school football program as a coach
  • Took Ohio State to new heights
  • Did his duty as an American citizen, joining the Navy and giving up his esteemed position at Ohio State
  • Dominated the AAFC so badly that interest waned from lack of competition
  • Entered the NFL with the same team and continued his domination
  • Only person to help found 2 separate NFL franchises
  • Most importantly, pioneered integrating black players into the league

"Paul Brown integrated pro football without uttering a single word about integration. He just went out, signed a bunch of great black athletes, and started kicking butt. That's how you do it. You don't talk about it. Paul never said one word about race. But this was a time in sports when you'd play in some cities and the white players could stay at the nice hotel, but the blacks had to stay in the homes of some black families in town. But not with Paul. We always stayed in hotels that took the entire team. Again, he never said a word. But in his own way, the man integrated football the right way – and no one was going to stop him." - Jim Brown

Player Origins

Paul was born in Norwalk, Ohio in 1908 and grew up in Massillon, Ohio. As he entered Masillon Washington high school in the early 1920's, the professional football teams that existed at the time had dispersed. This put the football focus of the area squarely on the high school teams. Paul had played football as a child but was considered undersized to play in high school. That is, until a coach saw his determination and invited him to try out. Paul ended up becoming the starting QB for his Junior and Senior years, posting a 15-3 record in that time. He failed to catch on at Ohio State University and ended up being a successful QB for Miami University in Ohio. He posted a 14-3 record and was named to the All-Ohio small-college second team by the Associated Press. This tenacity and perseverance by Paul where he continued to succeed when others told him he'd fail would play a large role in not only his future, but the future of the entire state of Ohio.

Massillon Washington High School

Coaching Origins

As with most coaches, Paul got his start coaching at the high school level. Unlike most coaches, he went undefeated in his first season. After a successful second season, he seized the opportunity to return to Masillon Washington High School as their head coach. The team had performed poorly in the previous six years and Paul was tasked with fixing it. He started by instilling discipline. He fired an assistant who was tardy and demanded none of his players take a seat on the bench during a game. He also went against the convention at the time by valuing speed over strength. After inheriting a team that finished 2-6-2 and was only getting worse, Paul went on to post the following records:

  • 5-4-1
  • 8-2
  • 9-1
  • 10-0

His impact was clear to see. Steady, consistent improvement until achieving perfection. The hallmarks of his success were discipline and inclusiveness. While being wildly successful as a coach is great, our pride in Paul Brown also stems from him being a pioneer in helping break the race barrier in football. Most schools in his area rejected any black students while Paul actively recruited black players to the team. After posting that first perfect record, Paul would go on to win every game in the next five years except one. That one game was a result of members of the team contracted the flu. In 1939, Massillon Washington High and Waite High School from Toledo both went undefeated as they weren't scheduled to play eachother, and both claimed the championship for themselves. When they played eachother a year later, Paul's team won 28-0. Then, as a preseason scrimmage, Massillon beat the Kent State University Golden Flashes. His highschool team played a college team, and won 47-0. Another hallmark of his success was innovation. While coaching Massillon, Paul invented what every single professional, college, and high school team uses today, the playbook. A collection of the team's offensive, defensive and special teams plays in the form of a text book including teaching these plays in a classroom setting. He also was the first coach to send plays to the QB from the sidelines using hand signals. He finished his Massillon tenure with an 80-8-2 record, winning the state championship four times and the national championship five times, all while outscoring opponents 2,393 points to 168.

College Coaching

Paul secured the head coaching position at one of the most heralded college football programs in existence at only 33 years old. Given his high school success, the program felt they had no choice between Paul's public support and the loyalty of his top recruits. He quickly set off instilling the discipline and innovation that brought him success at the high school level. Adding yet another inovation, the 40 yard dash time. This is the metric still used for every football prospect today to measure their speed, most notably at the annual scouting combine. He determined 40 yards was how far a player would need to run to cover a punt, and therefore wasn't concerned with speed past that distance. His first year record at Ohio State was 6-2, finishing 2nd in their conference, 13 nationally by an AP poll, and Brown himself finished 4th in voting for Coach of the Year. As you may have guessed, he only improved on those marks in year two. It was the first time Ohio State was voted #1 by the AP poll as they finished with one loss. Once again, Paul's team was overcome with sickness that lead to a defeat. Ohio State finished #1 in the AP Poll for the first time, won their conference, and won their first national title. In Paul's third season in 1943, his team was decimated by the draft. He could only play 17-year-old recruits due to Ohio State's rules of not allowing trainees to play varsity sports. His biggest competitors, however, were allowed to play their trainees. His record fell to 3-6 that year.

Paul was then commisioned as lieutenant in the Navy at the Great Lakes Naval Training Station. Luckily for him, the war was coming to an end and he avoided active service and instead coached the Great Lakes football program. He went 9-2-1 in his first year where he took over late in the process and finsihed in the top 20 in the AP poll. Going into his second season at Great Lakes, a professional league called the All-American Football Conference (AAFC) was being created to compete with the already establised National Football League (NFL). The owner of the Cleveland franchise for the AAFC approached Paul with the highest football coaching salary in the country along with full authority over the team, a stake in the franchise and a stipend while Paul finished his military service. The only problem was, he was still considered Ohio State's head coach and was expected to return when his military service ended. In the face of the greatest opportunity ever afforded a football coach in history, Paul couldn't say no. He expressed sadness for leaving Ohio State, saying

"I couldn't turn down this deal in fairness to my family."

His second year at Great Lakes started 0-4-1 after his best players had been transferred to the West Coast with the war taking place in the Pacific. After they returned, he won the last six games in a row before heading off to Cleveland.

Brown in Cleveland

Professional Origins

As Cleveland assembled its team, the vast majority of players came from Paul's stops at Massillon, Ohio State, and Great Lakes. Notably, Bill Willis from Ohio State and Marion Motley from Great Lakes became two of the first black people to play professional football. Paul was put in charge of naming the squad. He denied requests to name the team "the Browns" after himself. However, after much debate, the owner went ahead and named the team "the Browns" even though Paul did not support it. Paul was so irked by the team being named after him that he invented a lie saying it was named after the boxer Joe Louis' nickname, "The Brown Bomber". Near his death, Paul admitted he fabricated the story because he hated that the team was named after him. Finally, he began working on a dynasty, saying

"I want to be what the New York Yankees are in baseball or Ben Hogan is in golf"

In his first year, he amassed a 12-2 record, conference and national championships, and set attendance records while doing so. Always the innovator, Paul introduced new formations and expanded on the forward pass en route to total league domination. He would go on to win the championship each of his first four years, including an undefeated season in 1948 which would go unmatched in pro football until 1972. His domination was so thorough, attendance and interest had waned in the league with fans citing guaranteed lopsided victories taking away the excitement. The AAFC then dissolved and the Browns merged with the NFL.

NFL - Cleveland Browns

Paul won his first NFL game 35-10 and it was against the two-time defending league champs. He then won the NFL championship that first year as well. Including his AAFC tenure, Paul would win 12 professional championships in a matter of 16 years. No other coach would win both a college and a pro football championship until the 1990's. Despite this unprecedented level of success in any professional sport, Paul's leadership began to come under fire in the late 1950's. It was a culmination of several factors, namely that television had lead to individual players becoming national superstars and a new owner had bought the team. The players had a public forum to voice any disagreements they might have with Paul and the new owner was just a few years older than the players, leading him to be more sympathetic to their voices than to the legendary football coach. Paul was eventually fired in 1963. The next five years would be spent away from the sport he loved so much, saying

"It was terrible. I had everything a man could want: leisure, enough money, a wonderful family. Yet with all that, I was eating my heart out."

Brown in Cincinnati

NFL - Cincinnati Bengals

Similar to the AAFC, the American Football League (AFL), was being established in the mid 1960's as a rival to the NFL and had decided to host a franchise in Cincinnati. Paul was the 3rd biggest investor, secured his role as head coach and general manager, and obtained full control of the team. The first two years were unsuccessful but Paul felt confident he was building a good core to propel the team forward. In 1970, the AFL merged with the NFL, and Paul found himself back in the league he had been unceremoniously exiled from. His starting QB from the 1970 season sustained a terrible injury. Paul and his offensive coordinator, Bill Walsh, had to develop a different offense around the limitations of the backup QB. The backup had a weak arm compared to the starter but could reliably deliver shorter passes accurately. That's how the West Coast offense was born. It's so named because Bill Walsh would make it famous as he won Super Bowls with the 49ers in San Francisco. As for Paul, he could never recapture the magic from his earlier coaching days, and failed to pass the first round of the playoffs in any of his remaining years as the coach. He announced his retirement from coaching in 1975, at the age of 67. The game had finally passed him by. He remained the general manager until his passing in 1991. In that time the Bengals would remain relatively competitive, even reaching two Super Bowls, before the team was handed over to his son, Mike Brown. Sam Wyche, the Bengals coach during Paul's final days, detailed his final meeting with Paul, saying

“I went to his home. Paul was very weak. He had the oxygen in the nostrils and you could tell he was very low. The first thing he says to me is, ‘Now, Sam, you make sure you tell the team to do this, do this and do that.’ All football. He wasn’t worried about any sympathy from anybody. He didn’t bring up his health. He was worried about his football team to the very last.”
